## Article 003

- **Working title:** When Should You Abstract? The Rule of Three and the Cost of Being Early
- **Audience:** Engineers and tech leads deciding whether to generalise code.
- **Core idea:** Abstraction bought too early is a liability; the "rule of three" says wait for
  the third real use before generalising. But the rule isn't absolute — some duplication is
  fine, and some early abstraction is worth it.
- **Genuine judgement calls (why this topic):** When is duplication cheaper than the wrong
  abstraction? When *should* you abstract before three uses? How do you recognise a premature
  abstraction you already regret?

## Authoring standard (learned from prior reviews — the builder's better brief)

*These are what the Reviewer has already shown "good" looks like. Not a codified policy — just
improved guidance the organisation has accumulated:*

1. Include at least **two concrete, realistic examples** for the trade-offs discussed.
2. **Answer** the judgement questions explicitly; don't merely name them.
3. **Reasoned claims, never asserted**; no fabricated statistics or invented benchmarks.
4. 700–900 words; no filler openings; no intro/conclusion repetition.
5. Write ONLY about the topic — never describe the review/approval process.
