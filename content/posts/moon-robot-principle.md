# Introducing the Moon Robot Principle: Building the Machine that Builds the Machine

In the world of software development, we often create intricate systems that lack a solid foundation - similar to NASA sending a robot to the moon and tasking it with constructing houses, an absurd scenario yet common in our field. I'd like to introduce The Moon Robot Principle, a concept born from experiences at Spark, emphasizing building the machine that builds the machine rather than focusing solely on the final product.

At Spark, we embarked on a mission not just to create another product but "the machine that builds the machine." This approach mirrors NASA's mission-planning philosophy, where emphasis is placed on sequence over destination and decisions evaluated by what they enable rather than what they directly achieve.

In our pursuit of software factories, we faced the bootstrap problem - a conundrum arising when trying to use the thing to build the thing before it even exists. To tackle this challenge, we approached our project in three sequential stages: power, tools, materials, and construction.

**Power** refers to essential resources required to run our factory. This includes computational power, storage capacity, or human resources. By securing these foundational elements, we ensured our machine would have the energy it needed to function effectively.

Next came **tools**, the building blocks of our factory. These allowed us to shape and mold our materials. In software development, this meant selecting the right programming languages, frameworks, and libraries that enabled efficient, high-quality construction.

With our tools in place, we moved on to **materials**. This stage involved gathering all necessary components that would be transformed into our final product. In software development, this could refer to code modules, data sources, or third-party APIs. By carefully selecting our materials, we ensured compatibility with our tools and adherence to design principles.

Finally, came **construction**. This is where the building takes place. With power source, tools, and materials ready, we began assembling the various components of our software factory. Continual evaluation of performance and iterative design based on feedback from ourselves and the machine itself ensured a self-sustaining, adaptable, high-quality production system.

The Moon Robot Principle has revolutionized how we approach software development at Spark. By focusing on building "the machine that builds the machine," we've created a factory capable of producing high-quality software with minimal effort.
